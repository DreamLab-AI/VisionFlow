- [[Death of the Internet]]
	- Martin Heidegger's philosophy, particularly his later work on technology, offers a profound lens through which to understand the "ontological threat" posed by AI. It's not merely a practical or ethical concern, but a fundamental challenge to human being and our way of being-in-the-world.
	  Here's a breakdown of the ontological threat from a Heideggerian perspective:
	   * Technology as a "Way of Revealing" (Poiesis vs. Enframing):
	     * For Heidegger, technology is not just a collection of tools or machines. Its essence lies in a "way of revealing" (aletheia) reality.
	     * Historically, this revealing was often understood as a "bringing forth" (poiesis), where something emerged into unconcealment, like a craftsman shaping wood. There was a sense of respecting the inherent nature of things.
	     * The Threat: Modern Technology as "Enframing" (Gestell): Modern technology, according to Heidegger, operates through "Enframing" (Gestell). This is a mode of revealing where everything in the world, including humans, is reduced to a "standing-reserve" (Bestand) – a raw material or resource to be optimized, ordered, and exploited for efficiency and control.
	     * AI as the Culmination of Enframing: AI, particularly in its advanced forms like Large Language Models and Artificial General Intelligence, is seen by some as the ultimate manifestation of Enframing. It aims to quantify, process, and optimize information and even human behavior, reducing complex phenomena to calculable data points.
	   * The Loss of "World" and "Dasein's" Authenticity:
	     * Heidegger's central concept is "Dasein," which refers to human existence as "being-in-the-world." Dasein is characterized by its capacity for understanding, meaning-making, and being aware of its own finitude (mortality).
	     * The Threat: Enframing, as embodied by advanced AI, threatens Dasein's ability to truly "be-in-the-world."
	       * Reduction of Meaning: When everything becomes a standing-reserve, the inherent meaning and richness of the world can be obscured. The world is no longer encountered as something with intrinsic value, but as something to be managed and controlled.
	       * Loss of Authentic Self: If humans themselves are increasingly viewed as resources to be optimized (e.g., through data analytics, predictive algorithms), our capacity for authentic choice, genuine care (Sorge), and grappling with existential questions can diminish. We become less like "Dasein" and more like "Bestand."
	       * Obscuring Being: The danger is not that technology itself is bad, but that Enframing becomes the only way of revealing, thereby obscuring other ways of understanding Being. We lose sight of the possibility of a different relationship with ourselves, others, and the world.
	   * The Illusion of Control and the "Danger of the Danger":
	     * While humans initially believe they are in control of technology, Heidegger argues that modern technology's essence (Enframing) is not something we simply wield. Rather, it shapes us and our thinking.
	     * The Threat: With AI, the illusion of control becomes even more pronounced. We might believe we are directing AI, but the very logic of optimization and efficiency that AI embodies can subtly, yet profoundly, redefine human goals, values, and even what it means to think. The "danger of the danger" is not the catastrophic outcome, but the blindness to the ongoing, subtle transformation of our being.
	   * AI and the Question of Consciousness/Authenticity:
	     * A key distinction in Heidegger's philosophy is between Dasein and mere "present-at-hand" things. AI, even highly sophisticated AI, is often argued to remain "present-at-hand." It lacks consciousness, self-awareness, mood, genuine care, and the awareness of its own mortality – all hallmarks of Dasein.
	     * The Threat: The danger is not that AI becomes Dasein, but that in attempting to mimic or even surpass human capabilities, we might mistakenly reduce human intelligence and being to what AI can do, thereby devaluing the unique ontological depth of human existence.
	  In essence, the ontological threat of AI, when viewed through a Heideggerian lens, is not about robots taking over the world in a sci-fi dystopia. It's about a more insidious process where the pervasive logic of technological optimization (Enframing) fundamentally alters our relationship to Being, reduces our understanding of ourselves and the world to mere resources, and potentially obscures the very possibility of authentic human existence. The challenge, then, is not to simply control AI, but to find a "free relation" to technology that allows other ways of revealing Being to emerge.
