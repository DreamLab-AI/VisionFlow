## Academic Context

- Brief contextual overview
	- Non-fungible tokens (NFTs) are cryptographic assets recorded on distributed ledgers, most commonly blockchains, that represent unique ownership or rights to digital or physical items
	- The concept builds on foundational work in cryptography, distributed systems, and digital rights management, with roots in early blockchain research and the development of smart contracts
	- NFTs differ from fungible cryptocurrencies (such as Bitcoin or Ethereum) in that each token is distinct and not interchangeable with another of equal value

- Key developments and current state
	- NFTs have evolved from experimental digital collectibles to a broader class of assets used in art, gaming, identity, and supply chain management
	- The market has matured, with a shift from speculative trading to more practical applications and integration into established industries

- Academic foundations
	- The theoretical basis for NFTs lies in blockchain technology, cryptographic hashing, and smart contract programming
	- Early academic work includes research on digital scarcity, tokenisation, and the economic implications of non-fungible digital assets

## Current Landscape (2025)

- Industry adoption and implementations
	- NFTs are used across a range of sectors including art, gaming, music, fashion, and real estate
	- Major platforms such as OpenSea, Rarible, and Foundation continue to support NFT marketplaces, with increasing integration into mainstream e-commerce and digital identity systems
	- In the UK, organisations like the British Museum and the National Gallery have explored NFTs for digital art and cultural heritage projects

- Notable organisations and platforms
	- OpenSea, Rarible, Foundation, and SuperRare are leading global NFT marketplaces
	- UK-based platforms such as KnownOrigin and Mintable have gained traction, particularly in the digital art and collectibles space

- UK and North England examples where relevant
	- The University of Manchester has hosted research projects on blockchain and digital ownership, including NFT applications in cultural heritage
	- Leeds-based artists and collectives have experimented with NFTs for digital art exhibitions and community-driven projects
	- Newcastle and Sheffield have seen local initiatives using NFTs for digital identity and supply chain transparency in regional industries

- Technical capabilities and limitations
	- NFTs provide a secure, immutable record of ownership and provenance, but do not inherently confer copyright or intellectual property rights
	- The technology is limited by scalability, energy consumption (for proof-of-work blockchains), and the potential for fraud or misrepresentation
	- Most NFTs reference external digital files rather than storing them on the blockchain, which can lead to issues with file persistence and accessibility

- Standards and frameworks
	- Common NFT standards include ERC-721 and ERC-1155 on the Ethereum blockchain, with emerging standards for other blockchains and use cases
	- Interoperability and cross-chain compatibility remain active areas of development
