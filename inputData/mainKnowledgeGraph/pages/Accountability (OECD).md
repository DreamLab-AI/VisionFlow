## Accountability (OECD)

Accountability (OECD) refers to organisations and individuals developing, deploying or operating ai systems should be accountable for their proper functioning in accordance with oecd ai principles and applicable legal frameworks, based on their roles, context and ability to act.

- Global adoption and policy implementation
  - Over 1000 policy initiatives across more than 70 jurisdictions follow OECD AI Principles as of May 2023[3]
  - OECD member countries expected to actively support and implement principles
  - Framework significantly influenced EU AI Act and NIST AI Risk Management Framework[1]
- Accountability operationalisation across organisations
  - Defining clear responsibility chains for AI system development, deployment and operation
  - Establishing who bears accountability based on roles and contextual factors
  - Integrating accountability into broader AI governance structures and risk management frameworks[2]
- Technical implementation considerations
  - Accountability mechanisms must account for systems that evolve post-deployment
  - Generative AI systems present particular accountability challenges requiring clarified definitions
  - Term "AI actors" intentionally broad—not defined by territory or sector, allowing flexible implementation[5]
- UK and North England context
  - UK government adopts OECD definitions and classifications for harmonised governance
  - Manchester, Leeds, Newcastle and Sheffield emerging as AI innovation hubs with growing accountability frameworks in place
  - Financial services sector in Leeds and Manchester increasingly implementing OECD-aligned accountability structures
  - NHS trusts across North England integrating accountability principles into AI deployment in clinical settings

## Research & Literature

- OECD (2024). *Recommendation of the Council on Artificial Intelligence*. Legal Instruments OECD-LEGAL-0449. Updated revision clarifying AI system definitions and accountability requirements.[7]
- OECD (2023). *AI Principles: Revised Definition of AI Systems*. Aligned with technological evolution to provide foundation for government legislation and regulation.[6]
- UNESCO (2023). *Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence*. Complementary framework emphasising human responsibility and accountability, with eleven key policy action areas.[4]
- Bradley Insights (2025). *Global AI Governance: Five Key Frameworks Explained*. Comparative analysis of OECD, UNESCO, NIST, ISO 42001 and IEEE 7000 frameworks.[1]

- White & Case LLP (2025). *AI Watch: Global Regulatory Tracker*. Analysis of OECD accountability mechanisms and implementation challenges across jurisdictions.[5]

## Future Directions

- Evolving accountability mechanisms
  - Anticipated refinement of "AI actors" definition to address emerging actor categories (e.g., foundation model developers, API providers)
  - Development of sector-specific accountability guidance whilst maintaining cross-sector principles
  - Integration of accountability with emerging risk-based regulatory approaches
- Technical and governance challenges
  - Accountability attribution in complex multi-actor AI supply chains remains unresolved
  - Balancing accountability requirements with innovation incentives—a tension the OECD explicitly seeks to preserve[5]
  - Defining accountability boundaries for systems exhibiting emergent behaviours post-deployment
- Research priorities
  - Empirical assessment of accountability framework effectiveness across jurisdictions
  - Comparative analysis of implementation approaches (principles-based versus prescriptive regulation)
  - Development of accountability metrics and assessment tools aligned with OECD framework
  - Investigation of accountability mechanisms in decentralised and open-source AI development contexts
